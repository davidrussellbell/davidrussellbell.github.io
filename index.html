Research

Machine Learning, Chemistry, and Physics

Machine learning techniques comprise a diverse compendium of methods that have strengths and weaknesses but are overall well-suited to drive many discoveries in chemistry and physics. I employ machine learning and classical simulation techniques to model and interrogate native and synthetic materials, targeting fascinating unsolved systems in the physical and life sciences.

  • More

Multiscale Modeling

The strength of multiscale modeling is not only reducing computational expense, but simplifying the physical system to its basic animus. I have employed multiscale modeling techniques at various lengthscales for generative modeling of structure and thermodynamic information.

  • More

Antigen Immunotherapies

Immune complexes are especially tricky, typified by catch bonds and selection against minimum free energy states. I have studied at length the MHC-II-TCR immune complex, which is responsible for activation of immune T-cells for a variety of different effects, including autoimmune disease.

  • More

Nanomaterials

Nanomaterials such as graphene and quantum dots are well suited for game-changing application to energy and biomedical fields. I have explored CdSe quantum dot aggregation and interaction with a widespread protein domain to try and understand quantum dot toxicity.

  • More

Nucleic Acids and Interactions

Nucleic acid structure, dynamics, and interactions also prove a formidable challenge to model, primarily because nucleic acids are highly charged polymers. I have built a multiscale model to predict nucleic acid structure using statistical potentials and experimental free energy information. I have also built a 3-D RNA structure that I used to predict RNA-protein binding affinity and design higher-affinity RNAs with verification by calorimetry experiments.
